How to choose the right ice cream maker

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best ice cream maker for you depends on your budget, patience, and kitchen counter space. If you want instant gratification and make ice cream often, get a compressor machine. If you’re an occasional maker with freezer space, a freezer-bowl model offers the best value. Hand-crank models are for nostalgia or off-grid fun.

What type of ice cream maker should I buy?

There are three main types: compressor machines, freezer-bowl machines, and hand-crank or manual models. Compressor machines have their own built-in freezer, so you can make batch after batch without waiting. They tend to be the most expensive and the heaviest, but they offer the most consistent results and the least fuss, just pour in your base and turn it on. Freezer-bowl models are the most popular home option. You freeze the bowl solid (usually 12 to 24 hours), then churn for 20 to 30 minutes. They’re very affordable and bulky only when the bowl is in the freezer. The trade-off is that you need to plan ahead and have room in your freezer for the bowl. Hand-crank or ice-and-salt machines are the old-school choice. They require ice, rock salt, and elbow grease. They produce dense, old-fashioned ice cream that some people swear by. They’re great for camping or kids’ science projects, but they’re a labor of love for everyday use.

How much capacity do I really need?

Most home ice cream makers produce between 1 and 2 quarts of finished ice cream. A 1-quart machine is fine for two people or small batches of different flavors. A 1.5- or 2-quart machine works well for a family of four or for entertaining. Larger compressor models often offer 2 quarts or more, but the bowl itself won’t hold more than about 1.5 quarts of base because the ice cream expands during churning. If you frequently host parties or have a big family, consider a larger compressor machine. For occasional use, a 1.5-quart freezer-bowl model is a smart middle ground. Remember, you can always make multiple batches, but you can’t overfill the bowl, check the maximum fill line on any machine you’re considering.

What features make the creamiest ice cream?

Creaminess comes down to two things: the recipe and the churning action. A good ice cream maker should have a powerful motor and a paddle that scrapes the frozen mixture from the sides of the bowl. This creates tiny ice crystals and incorporates air (called overrun). Machines with a built-in compressor tend to churn colder and faster, which can produce a silkier texture with less iciness. Some high-end models include a pre-cool function or a “gelato” mode that churns slower for a denser Italian-style dessert. A clear lid is a nice bonus so you can watch the consistency without stopping the machine. For freezer-bowl models, a double-insulated bowl is better at maintaining temperature, but even basic designs work well if you pre-freeze long enough.

How much prep and cleanup are involved?

Prep time varies wildly. With a compressor machine, you can literally add your base and press start, no pre-freezing required. Cleanup is usually straightforward because the bowl and paddle are easy to remove. Many modern models have dishwasher-safe parts, but double-check before buying. Freezer-bowl machines demand planning: you need to freeze the bowl for at least 12 hours upside down in the coldest part of your freezer. That means you can’t decide on a whim to make ice cream unless you keep the bowl ready. After churning, you typically need to transfer the soft-serve ice cream to a container and freeze it for a few hours to firm up. Hand-crank models involve messing with ice and salt, and the cleanup is more involved.

Should I get a machine that also makes sorbet or gelato?

Almost all ice cream makers can handle sorbet and frozen yogurt, the base just changes. Many machines market themselves as “ice cream & gelato” models, but the difference is often just a slower churn speed or a different paddle. If you love authentic Italian gelato, look for a machine that explicitly offers a gelato mode with a lower paddle RPM. Some also include a sorbet paddle that incorporates less air for a denser fruit sorbet. However, any decent freezer-bowl or compressor machine will produce good sorbet and frozen yogurt. The real differentiator is temperature control: compressor machines let you fine-tune the final product temperature, which is helpful for sorbets that freeze harder. If you only plan to make standard ice cream, you won’t miss those extras.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need a compressor machine to get good results?

No. A quality freezer-bowl machine can produce excellent ice cream as long as you pre-freeze the bowl thoroughly and use a well-chilled base. Compressor machines are best for frequent users who want the convenience of batch-after-batch without waiting and value consistent temperature control.

How long does it take to make ice cream from start to finish?

With a freezer-bowl machine, expect about 30 minutes of churning plus 2 to 4 hours in the freezer to harden. With a compressor machine, you can eat soft-serve right after churning (about 20–30 minutes) and freeze it for firmer scoops. Don’t forget the 12–24 hour pre-freeze for the bowl if you go that route.

Can I use any recipe in my ice cream maker?

Yes, but you may need to adjust liquid and sugar ratios. Very high-fat recipes (like Philadelphia-style) work in all machines. Low-fat or dairy-free bases sometimes require a different approach, like adding a stabilizer, to avoid iciness. Most consumer machines handle standard custard-based or simple cream-and-milk recipes just fine.

How do I clean an ice cream maker properly?

Always unplug the machine and never submerge the motor base. The bowl and paddle can usually be washed with warm soapy water and a non-abrasive sponge. Many freezer-bowl and compressor bowls have non-stick coatings, so avoid metal utensils. Check if parts are dishwasher-safe, but hand-washing is gentler on the coating.

Is a built-in freezer bowl better than a removable one?

Built-in bowls are rare in home machines; almost all are removable. Removable bowls are easier to clean and can be stored in the freezer separately. The only “built-in” style is a compressor machine where the bowl stays inside the unit, but that’s a different category entirely. Both designs work well; just make sure the bowl is sturdy and seals well during freezing.

What is overrun and why does it matter?

Overrun is the amount of air whipped into the ice cream during churning. High overrun (like in cheap commercial ice cream) makes it fluffy and melts quickly. Low overrun gives a dense, rich, premium texture. Good home machines let you control overrun by churning just until the ice cream thickens. Compressor machines often produce lower overrun, which is ideal for creamy results.

In shortWhether you choose a freezer-bowl workhorse or a compressor luxury model, the best ice cream maker is the one you’ll actually use. Match the machine to your habit, not to a fantasy of making ice cream every weekend. Once you’ve got a churner that fits your kitchen and your schedule, a world of homemade flavors is at your fingertips.
